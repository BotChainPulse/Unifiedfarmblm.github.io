Chicken Feeding Guide: What to Feed Your Layers and Broilers in Uganda

Feeding is the single largest expense in poultry farming, making up 60-70% of your total costs. Getting feeding right means the difference between profit and loss. This guide covers everything you need to know about feeding chickens in Uganda, from commercial feeds to homemade recipes using locally available ingredients.

Understanding Chicken Nutritional Needs

Chickens need six main nutrients: proteins, carbohydrates, fats, vitamins, minerals, and water. The amounts needed change depending on the age and purpose of the bird. A day-old chick has very different needs from a laying hen or a broiler ready for market.

Protein Requirements by Age

Protein is essential for growth, feather development, and egg production. Here is what different chickens need:

Age / Type Protein Needed Purpose
0-8 weeks (Starter) 20-22% Fast growth and organ development
8-18 weeks (Grower) 16-18% Steady growth without excess fat
18+ weeks - Laying (Layer) 16-18% Egg production support
0-6 weeks (Broiler Starter) 22-24% Maximum growth rate
6+ weeks (Broiler Finisher) 18-20% Final weight gain

Homemade Feed Recipes Using Local Ingredients

Many successful farmers in Uganda mix their own feed to reduce costs. Here are proven recipes using locally available materials.

Layer Feed Recipe (For Egg-Producing Hens)

Ingredient Amount Purpose
Maize bran 50 kg Energy source
Cotton seed cake 15 kg Protein
Silver fish (mukene) 10 kg Protein and calcium
Sunflower seed cake 8 kg Protein and fats
Shells or bone meal 5 kg Calcium for eggshells
Premix 2 kg Vitamins and minerals
Salt 0.5 kg Mineral balance

Instructions:

  1. Grind all ingredients to similar particle size
  2. Mix thoroughly for at least 15 minutes
  3. Store in a dry place away from sunlight
  4. Use within 2 weeks for best freshness

This homemade layer feed costs approximately 30-40% less than commercial alternatives.

Feeding Schedule by Age

Day-Old Chicks to 2 Weeks

  • Feed starter mash continuously (ad libitum)
  • Provide fresh, clean water at all times
  • Use shallow chick feeders
  • Expected feed intake: 10-20 grams per chick per day

2 to 8 Weeks

  • Continue starter mash
  • Gradually introduce grower mash from week 6
  • Expected feed intake: 30-60 grams per bird per day

18+ Weeks - Laying Hens

  • Switch to layer mash 2 weeks before first eggs expected
  • Provide extra calcium in a separate container
  • Expected feed intake: 100-120 grams per bird per day
  • A laying hen eats approximately 110-130 kg of feed per year

Broilers

  • Week 1-4: Broiler starter, ad libitum
  • Week 5-8: Broiler finisher, ad libitum
  • A broiler eats approximately 3.5-4 kg of feed to reach 2 kg live weight

Water: The Forgotten Nutrient

Water is the most important nutrient. A chicken will die of thirst before it dies of hunger.

Age / Type Water Per Day
Day-old chicks 20-40 ml
Growing birds 100-200 ml
Laying hens 200-400 ml
Broilers 200-350 ml

In Uganda's hot climate, water needs can double. Always provide fresh, clean water.

Common Feeding Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Irregular feeding times: Chickens thrive on routine. Feed at the same times daily.
  2. Poor feed storage: Store in sealed containers in a cool, dry place. Mold kills.
  3. Not adjusting for weather: Feed early morning and evening in hot weather.
  4. Overfeeding treats: Kitchen scraps should be maximum 10-15% of daily intake.
  5. Ignoring feed conversion: Track feed eaten versus eggs produced or weight gained.

Conclusion

Feeding your chickens properly is the foundation of profitable poultry farming. Whether you use commercial feeds or homemade mixtures, provide the right nutrients at the right time. Track your costs carefully — the farmers who succeed know exactly how much they spend and earn.
