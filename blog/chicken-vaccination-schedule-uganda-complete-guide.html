Understanding Chicken Vaccination Schedules in Uganda: Complete 2026 Guide

Keeping your chickens healthy is the most important part of poultry farming. In Uganda, where diseases like Newcastle Disease and Gumboro are common, vaccination is not optional — it is essential. This guide will walk you through everything you need to know about vaccinating your chickens, from day-old chicks to mature layers.

Why Vaccination Matters in Uganda

Uganda's warm climate is perfect for raising chickens, but it also creates ideal conditions for disease spread. Many small-scale farmers lose entire flocks because they skip vaccinations or follow the wrong schedule. A proper vaccination program costs very little compared to the value of your birds.

Vaccination protects your investment. A single outbreak of Newcastle Disease can wipe out 90% of an unvaccinated flock. For a farmer with 100 birds, that is a devastating loss. Vaccination gives your chickens immunity, allowing them to fight off dangerous diseases.

Essential Vaccines for Chickens in Uganda

1. Marek's Disease Vaccine

When to give: Day 1 (at the hatchery or immediately after receiving chicks)
How: Subcutaneous injection under the skin at the back of the neck
Why: Marek's Disease causes paralysis and tumors. Once symptoms appear, there is no treatment. This vaccine must be given on the first day of life.
Cost: Usually included if you buy vaccinated chicks from a reputable supplier like Ugachick or Biyinzika Poultry International.

Tip: Always ask your chick supplier if Marek's vaccination has been done before purchase. If not, arrange for immediate vaccination.

2. Newcastle Disease Vaccine (NDV)

Newcastle Disease is the biggest threat to poultry in Uganda. It spreads rapidly and can kill entire flocks within days. Multiple doses are needed throughout the bird's life.

Vaccination Schedule:

  • Day 1-7: Newcastle Disease + Infectious Bronchitis (combined vaccine) — eye drop
  • Day 14-21: Newcastle Disease Lasota strain — drinking water or eye drop
  • Week 6-8: Newcastle Disease Komarov or LaSota booster — drinking water
  • Every 3-4 months: Booster doses for layers and breeders

Signs of Newcastle Disease:

  • Sudden death with no warning
  • Green diarrhea
  • Twisted necks (torticollis)
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Drop in egg production
  • Swelling around the eyes and neck

Cost: UGX 5,000 - 15,000 per 100 doses depending on the supplier

3. Gumboro (Infectious Bursal Disease) Vaccine

Gumboro attacks the immune system of young chicks, making them vulnerable to every other disease. It is most deadly between 3 and 6 weeks of age.

Vaccination Schedule:

  • Day 10-14: First Gumboro vaccination — drinking water
  • Day 21-28: Second Gumboro vaccination (booster) — drinking water

Signs of Gumboro:

  • Diarrhea with white, watery droppings
  • Depression and ruffled feathers
  • Pecking at the vent area
  • Sudden death in apparently healthy chicks
  • Buried heads and huddling together

Cost: UGX 8,000 - 20,000 per 100 doses

4. Fowl Typhoid Vaccine

Fowl Typhoid is a bacterial infection that affects chickens of all ages. It causes severe illness and death, especially in growing birds.

Vaccination Schedule:

  • Week 6-8: First Fowl Typhoid vaccination
  • Week 16-18: Booster dose for layers

Signs of Fowl Typhoid:

  • Yellow-green diarrhea
  • Pale comb and wattles
  • Increased thirst
  • Reduced appetite
  • Drop in egg production

Cost: UGX 10,000 - 25,000 per 100 doses

5. Fowl Pox Vaccine

Fowl Pox causes scabby sores on the comb, wattles, and skin. In severe cases, it can cause death, especially in young birds.

Vaccination Schedule:

  • Week 6-10: Wing web stab method
  • Repeat every 6 months for birds kept in mosquito-prone areas

Signs of Fowl Pox:

  • Scabby lesions on comb, wattles, and face
  • Difficulty eating if lesions are in the mouth
  • Reduced egg production
  • Stunted growth in young birds

Cost: UGX 8,000 - 18,000 per 100 doses

Where to Buy Vaccines in Uganda

You can purchase quality vaccines from:

  • Ugachick Poultry Breeders — Kampala and major towns
  • Biyinzika Poultry International — Multiple locations
  • National Animal Genetic Resources Centre and Data Bank (NAGRC&DB) — Entebbe
  • Local veterinary pharmacies in Kampala, Jinja, Mbarara, and Gulu
  • Authorized agro-input dealers in your district

Important: Always buy from authorized dealers. Fake or expired vaccines are common in the market. Check the expiry date and storage conditions. Vaccines must be kept cold — if they were stored at room temperature, they are useless.

How to Administer Vaccines at Home

Eye Drop Method

  1. Hold the chick firmly but gently
  2. Place one drop in the eye
  3. Wait for the bird to blink before releasing
  4. Use a clean dropper for each batch

Drinking Water Method

  1. Remove water the night before vaccination
  2. Mix the vaccine in clean, cool water (not chlorinated)
  3. Add skim milk or vaccine stabilizer to protect the virus
  4. Give only vaccinated water for 2-4 hours
  5. Protect from sunlight — cover the drinkers

Injection Method

  1. Use a new sterile needle for each bird
  2. Inject under the skin, not into muscle (for subcutaneous)
  3. For wing web, stab through the webbing of the wing
  4. Dispose of needles safely after use

Common Vaccination Mistakes to Avoid

1. Vaccinating sick birds: Only vaccinate healthy chickens. Sick birds will not develop proper immunity and may get worse.

2. Using expired vaccines: Always check the expiry date. Expired vaccines are ineffective.

3. Wrong storage: Most vaccines need refrigeration at 2-8°C. Never freeze vaccines unless specifically instructed.

4. Using chlorinated water: Chlorine kills the live virus in vaccines. Use boiled and cooled water, or add skim milk.

5. Skipping booster doses: One dose is rarely enough. Follow the complete schedule with all boosters.

6. Vaccinating during extreme heat: Do vaccinations early in the morning when temperatures are cooler.

Cost of Full Vaccination Program

For a flock of 100 chickens, expect to spend approximately UGX 50,000 - 100,000 on vaccines for the complete program. This is a small investment compared to losing your entire flock to disease.

Final Thoughts

Vaccination is the foundation of successful poultry farming in Uganda. It is far cheaper and easier to prevent disease than to treat it. Follow this schedule carefully, buy from reputable suppliers, and keep good records of all vaccinations given.

Healthy chickens grow faster, produce more eggs, and make more profit. Make vaccination your top priority.
