Layer Farming in Uganda: Guide to 300+ Eggs Per Year

Layer farming is the backbone of sustainable poultry income. While broilers give you a quick payday every 8 weeks, layers provide daily income for 18–24 months straight. At Unified Farm BLM, our layer flock of 80 ISA Brown hens produces 65–72 eggs every single day — a reliable revenue stream that covers our entire farm's operating costs. This guide shows you exactly how to build a profitable layer operation in Uganda.

Why Layer Farming Makes Sense in Uganda

  • Daily income: Eggs are collected every morning — cash flow 7 days a week.
  • High demand: Every household, restaurant, and school in Uganda uses eggs.
  • Long production cycle: Good layers produce for 18–24 months.
  • Low labor: Once set up, layers require only 30–45 minutes of care per day.
  • Multiple revenue streams: Eggs, spent hens, and manure all generate income.
Egg consumption in Uganda: The average Ugandan consumes 23 eggs per year — far below the recommended 104 eggs. This gap represents enormous market potential for layer farmers.

The Layer Production Cycle: Month by Month

Months 1–4: Rearing Phase (No eggs yet)

Day-old layer chicks need specialized care for their first 18 weeks. During this period, they grow from 40 grams to 1.5–1.8 kg and develop the bodily systems needed for egg production.

  • Feed: Chick starter (0–8 weeks) then grower pellets (8–18 weeks)
  • Protein requirement: 18–20% protein during starter phase, 16–18% during grower
  • Vaccination: Complete Newcastle, Gumboro, Fowl Pox, and Bronchitis vaccines
  • Lighting: 16 hours of light daily starting at week 16 stimulates egg production
  • Mortality target: Under 5% during rearing
Rearing cost per bird (18 weeks): UGX 12,000 – 15,000 including feed, vaccines, and medication. This is your investment before any egg income begins.

Month 5: Point of Lay — The First Eggs

At 18–20 weeks, hens begin laying their first eggs. These "pullet eggs" are small (45–50 grams) but signal that production is starting. Egg size increases steadily over the next 6–8 weeks.

  • Week 18–20: 20–30% of hens start laying (small eggs)
  • Week 21–24: 60–80% production rate (medium eggs)
  • Week 25+: 85–95% peak production (large eggs)

Months 6–14: Peak Production Phase

This is the golden period. Well-managed ISA Brown hens produce 300+ eggs in their first year of laying. Key management practices during peak production:

  • Feed: Layer mash or pellets (16–18% protein, 3.5–4% calcium)
  • Calcium supplement: Provide crushed oyster shells or eggshells free-choice
  • Water: Never let water run out. A laying hen drinks 250–300ml daily.
  • Lighting: Maintain 16 hours of light throughout peak production
  • Nesting boxes: One box per 4–5 hens, lined with clean straw
  • Collection: Gather eggs 2–3 times daily to prevent breakage and soiling

Months 15–18: Declining Production

Egg production gradually declines by 1–2% per week after peak. By month 18, production drops to 65–75%. You must decide whether to keep the flock or replace them.

Months 18–24: End of Cycle

Most commercial farmers sell spent hens at 18 months when production falls below 65%. In Uganda, spent hens sell for UGX 12,000–18,000 each to local markets and restaurants.

5 Mistakes That Destroy Layer Profits

  1. Buying the wrong breed: Local chickens produce 40–60 eggs/year. Commercial layers produce 300+. The breed choice alone determines 80% of your egg output.
  2. Insufficient calcium: Without adequate calcium, hens produce soft-shelled or shell-less eggs that cannot be sold. Always provide calcium supplements.
  3. Inconsistent lighting: Layers need exactly 16 hours of light daily. Any disruption — power outages, dark coops — causes a drop in production that takes weeks to recover.
  4. Overcrowding: Each layer needs 0.15–0.2 square meters of floor space. Crowding causes stress, feather pecking, and disease.
  5. Ignoring egg quality: Dirty, cracked, or misshapen eggs sell for 30–50% less. Clean nesting boxes and gentle handling protect your premium prices.
