Newcastle Disease in Chickens: Symptoms, Prevention, and Treatment

Newcastle Disease is the most devastating poultry disease in Uganda. It spreads fast, kills quickly, and can wipe out an entire unvaccinated flock in a matter of days. Every poultry farmer in Uganda must understand this disease and know how to prevent it. This guide covers everything you need to protect your flock.

What is Newcastle Disease?

Newcastle Disease is caused by a virus called Paramyxovirus. It affects chickens of all ages and breeds. The virus attacks the respiratory, nervous, and digestive systems. It spreads through the air, contaminated feed and water, and contact with infected birds or their droppings.

In Uganda, Newcastle Disease outbreaks occur throughout the year but are most common during the dry seasons when birds are stressed and water sources are shared. The disease is so common that many farmers simply call it "the plague" or "kidera" in local languages.

Symptoms of Newcastle Disease

Newcastle Disease can show different symptoms depending on the strain and the bird's immune system. Watch for these warning signs:

Respiratory Signs

  • Gasping and difficulty breathing
  • Coughing and sneezing
  • Nasal discharge (runny nose)
  • Gurgling sounds when breathing

Nervous System Signs

  • Twisted necks (torticollis) — the most recognizable sign
  • Circling or walking backward
  • Tremors and paralysis of wings and legs
  • Drooping wings
  • Inability to stand or walk properly

Digestive Signs

  • Green, watery diarrhea
  • Complete loss of appetite
  • Increased thirst

Production Signs

  • Sudden, dramatic drop in egg production
  • Soft-shelled or shell-less eggs
  • Death with no prior symptoms in severe cases

How Newcastle Disease Spreads

Understanding how the virus spreads helps you block every pathway:

  1. Direct contact: Healthy birds touch infected birds or their droppings
  2. Contaminated feed and water: The virus survives in leftover feed and dirty water
  3. Airborne: The virus can travel short distances through the air
  4. Wild birds: Pigeons and other wild birds can carry the virus
  5. Human visitors: Shoes, clothes, and hands can carry the virus between farms
  6. Contaminated equipment: Feeders, drinkers, and transport crates spread the virus
  7. New birds: Unvaccinated chicks or birds from unknown sources bring infection

Prevention: The Only Reliable Strategy

There is no cure for Newcastle Disease. Prevention through vaccination is the only effective strategy. Here is the complete prevention plan:

Biosecurity Measures

  1. Quarantine new birds: Keep new chickens separate for 2 weeks before introducing them to your flock
  2. Control visitors: Limit who enters your chicken area. Provide foot baths with disinfectant
  3. Keep wild birds out: Cover your coop with wire mesh. Do not allow pigeons near your flock
  4. Clean equipment: Wash and disinfect feeders and drinkers weekly
  5. Don't share equipment: Never borrow feeders or crates from other farmers
  6. Dispose of dead birds properly: Bury or burn dead chickens immediately. Do not leave them exposed

Maintain Strong Immune Systems

  • Provide balanced nutrition with adequate vitamins
  • Keep stress levels low (no overcrowding, good ventilation)
  • Add vitamin supplements to water during stressful periods
  • Maintain clean, dry housing conditions

What to Do If You Suspect Newcastle Disease

If you see symptoms in your flock, act immediately. Every hour counts.

Immediate Actions

  1. Isolate sick birds immediately — move them to a separate area
  2. Call a veterinarian — proper diagnosis is essential
  3. Stop all visits — do not allow anyone near the coop
  4. Vaccinate healthy birds immediately — emergency vaccination can save the rest
  5. Disinfect everything — feeders, drinkers, floors, and equipment

Treatment Options

There is no cure for the viral infection itself. However, you can treat secondary bacterial infections and support recovery:

  • Antibiotics: Treat secondary bacterial infections (consult a vet for the right type)
  • Vitamins and electrolytes: Add to drinking water to support the immune system
  • Isolation and rest: Keep sick birds warm, quiet, and hydrated
  • Good nutrition: Provide easily digestible, high-quality feed

Important: Birds that recover from Newcastle Disease may remain carriers and shed the virus for weeks. Keep recovered birds separate for at least 4 weeks.
