How to Tell Egg Freshness, Predict Egg Color, and Master Hatching for Your Flock

Three powerful skills every poultry farmer needs: know if an egg is still good without cracking it, predict what color eggs a hen will lay before she starts, and hatch your own chicks at home without buying an expensive incubator. These are practical tricks that save money, reduce waste, and put you in control of your flock.

The Water Test: How Fresh Is That Egg?

You don't need to crack an egg to know if it's still good. This simple water test works every time and is especially useful in Uganda where refrigeration is not always reliable.

How It Works

As an egg ages, the air cell inside grows larger. A fresh egg has almost no air cell, so it sinks. An old egg has a large air cell, so it floats.

  • Freshly Laid Egg: Sinks to the bottom and lies flat on its side. Safe to eat and excellent for frying or boiling.
  • One Week Old: Sinks but stands up on one end. Still safe to eat, but better for baking or scrambling.
  • 2 to 3 Weeks Old: Sinks but stands vertically. The air cell is growing. Safe only if fully cooked (boiled, fried hard). Not good for soft preparations.
  • Old Egg (Do Not Eat): Floats to the surface. This egg has spoiled. Throw it away immediately. Do not crack it open — it will smell terrible and may contain harmful bacteria.
💡 Pro Tip: Store eggs pointed-end down. This keeps the yolk centered and the air cell at the top, which slows aging and preserves quality. If you sell eggs, include the laying date on the tray so customers know exactly how fresh they are.

Why This Matters for Your Business

Customers remember bad eggs. If someone buys a tray and finds one rotten egg, they will never buy from you again. The water test is your quality control tool:

  • Test every egg before you pack them for sale.
  • Never mix eggs from different collection days. Always sell oldest first.
  • If you store eggs without refrigeration, test them every 3 days and remove any that stand up vertically.
  • Label every tray with the collection date. This builds trust and justifies your price.

Predict Egg Color by Looking at the Hen's Ear

Here is a farmer's trick that seems like magic but is simple biology: you can predict the color of eggs a hen will lay by looking at the color of her earlobe.

The Rule

  • Red or Brown Earlobe: The hen will lay brown eggs (or tinted/cream eggs).
  • White or Pale Blue Earlobe: The hen will lay white eggs (or blue/green eggs).

Most hens in Uganda have red earlobes and lay brown eggs. White Leghorns and some commercial hybrids have white earlobes and lay white eggs. Some heritage breeds (like Araucanas) have blue earlobes and lay blue or green eggs.

Why This Matters

  • Buying hens: If you want brown eggs for your market, check the earlobes before you buy. No need to wait 4-6 months for the first egg to find out.
  • Breeding: If you want to preserve a specific egg color in your flock, select parent hens with the matching earlobe color.
  • Marketing: White eggs are rare in many Ugandan markets and can command a premium price. If you intentionally raise white-egg layers, you can charge slightly more per tray.
🎯 Marketing Tip: Some customers believe white eggs are "healthier" or "cleaner" because they are less common in local markets. Use this to your advantage — if you have white layers, market them as premium or specialty eggs and charge UGX 18,000–20,000 per tray instead of 15,000.

Hatching & Incubation: The Complete Guide

Why buy chicks from a hatchery when you can hatch your own? Home hatching puts you in control of your flock genetics, saves money on transport and markup, and lets you scale your farm at your own pace. This is the exact process used by successful breeders across Uganda.

Step 1: Parent Stock (The Foundation of Good Chicks)

Your chicks are only as good as the parents. Never hatch from random hens. Select your parent stock carefully:

  • Age: Use hens that are 8 months to 2 years old. Very young hens lay small eggs that hatch poorly. Very old hens lay eggs with weak shells and lower fertility.
  • Health: Only use hens that are fully vaccinated, parasite-free, and in excellent body condition. Sick parents produce weak chicks.
  • Production: Choose hens that lay consistently (5-6 eggs per week). High producers tend to pass that trait to their daughters.
  • Rooster ratio: Keep 1 rooster for every 8-10 hens. Too many roosters stress the hens and reduce fertility. Too few roosters means some eggs will not be fertilized.
⚠️ Do not hatch from hens that are recovering from illness, have been treated with antibiotics in the last 14 days, or show signs of weakness. Medication residues can affect embryo development and chick health.

Step 2: Selection of Hatching Eggs

Not every egg is suitable for hatching. Follow these rules strictly:

  • Shape: Choose eggs that are oval and symmetrical. Reject eggs that are too round, too long, or have odd shapes — they hatch poorly and the chicks are weak.
  • Shell quality: The shell must be smooth, clean, and free of cracks. Rough, pitted, or thin shells allow bacteria to enter and kill the embryo.
  • Size: Use medium to large eggs. Tiny eggs hatch weak chicks. Very large eggs often have double yolks and rarely hatch successfully.
  • Age of egg: Collect eggs daily. Store hatching eggs for no more than 7 days. After 10 days, fertility drops sharply. After 14 days, hatching success is extremely low.
  • Cleanliness: Never wash hatching eggs. Washing removes the natural protective coating (bloom) and allows bacteria to enter. If an egg is dirty, set it aside and do not hatch it. Keep nesting boxes clean so eggs stay clean.

Step 3: Disinfection & Storage of Eggs

Before incubation, store your selected eggs properly to preserve fertility:

  • Temperature: Store at 15-18°C. In Uganda, a cool room, underground storage, or a clay pot in a shaded place works well.
  • Humidity: 70-80% humidity. If your storage area is dry, place a shallow dish of water nearby.
  • Position: Store with the pointed end down. This keeps the air cell at the wide end and the yolk centered.
  • Turning: Turn stored eggs once daily (180 degrees). This prevents the yolk from sticking to the shell membrane. If you skip turning, the embryo will die.
  • Disinfection: If you are concerned about bacteria, wipe eggs gently with a cloth dampened with a weak solution of potassium permanganate or dilute iodine. Do not wash.

Step 4: Types of Hatching

There are two main ways to hatch eggs:

  • Natural (Broody Hen): A broody hen sits on the eggs and keeps them warm. This is free, low-maintenance, and the chicks learn from the mother. However, not all hens go broody, and some broody hens abandon the eggs.
  • Artificial Incubator: Uses controlled heat, humidity, and turning. More reliable for large batches and gives you control over the entire process. Recommended for serious farmers.
💡 Which is better? For 5-10 eggs, a broody hen is perfect. For 20-100+ eggs, you need an incubator. In Uganda, small Chinese-made incubators (36-96 eggs) cost UGX 300,000-800,000 and pay for themselves after 2-3 hatches.

Step 5: Parts of an Incubator & Loading Eggs

Before you load eggs, understand what your incubator does:

  • Heating element: Maintains the correct temperature (usually 37.5°C for forced-air incubators, 38.5°C for still-air).
  • Thermostat: Controls temperature automatically. Test it before loading eggs.
  • Humidity control: Water trays or sponges that keep moisture at the right level.
  • Turning mechanism: Rotates eggs automatically (essential) or you turn by hand 3-5 times daily.
  • Ventilation: Allows fresh air in and stale air out. Never block vents.

Loading eggs: Place eggs in the incubator with the pointed end slightly down (about 45 degrees). Leave space between eggs for air circulation. Do not overcrowd. Close the lid and do not open it for the first 24 hours while the temperature stabilizes.

Step 6: Incubation Settings (Days 1-18)

Parameter Days 1-18 Days 19-21 (Hatching)
Temperature 37.5°C (forced-air) or 38.5°C (still-air) 37.0°C (reduce slightly)
Humidity 50-55% 65-70%
Turning 3-5 times daily (automatic or manual) STOP turning
Ventilation Normal Increase slightly

Do not open the incubator frequently. Every time you open the lid, temperature and humidity drop, which stresses the embryos. Check the thermometer through the window if possible.

Step 7: Setting & Turning of Eggs (Days 1-18)

Turning is critical. If you do not turn eggs, the embryo will stick to the shell and die:

  • Turn eggs 3-5 times per day, every day.
  • Turn at roughly the same times each day (morning, noon, evening, before bed).
  • Mark one side with an X and the other with an O using a pencil. This makes it easy to see that you have turned them.
  • Turn gently. Rough handling can crack the shell or detach the embryo.
  • Stop turning on Day 18. The chicks are preparing to hatch and need to position themselves.

Step 8: Lockdown & Hatching (Days 19-21)

On Day 18, you enter "lockdown":

  • Stop turning the eggs completely.
  • Increase humidity to 65-70% by adding extra water trays or sponges. High humidity softens the shell so the chick can break through.
  • Reduce temperature slightly by 0.5°C.
  • Do not open the incubator until hatching is complete. Chicks can survive 24-48 hours after hatching without food or water — they absorb the yolk sac.
  • You may hear chirping and see the eggs rocking. This is normal. The chicks are working their way out.
🐣 Hatching Tip: If a chick has pipped (cracked the shell) but is struggling for more than 24 hours, you may assist carefully. Only intervene if the chick is alive and active, and only remove tiny pieces of shell. Do not pull the chick out — it is still attached to blood vessels. Most chicks will hatch on their own if you are patient.

Expected Hatching Rates

  • Excellent: 85-90% hatch rate (experienced farmer, good eggs, quality incubator)
  • Good: 75-85% hatch rate (minor issues with temperature or humidity)
  • Average: 60-75% (first-time hatching, older eggs, or less-than-ideal storage)
  • Poor: Below 60% (temperature problems, infertile eggs, bad storage, or disease)

Hatchery Biosecurity & Disease Prevention

Your incubator is a perfect breeding ground for bacteria if you are not careful. One dirty batch can contaminate every egg that follows:

  • Clean the incubator between every hatch. Remove all shells, dust, and waste. Wipe with a disinfectant solution (Jik, quaternary ammonium, or formalin).
  • Never mix eggs from different sources. Eggs from another farm may carry diseases your flock has not been exposed to.
  • Wash your hands before handling eggs. Human bacteria can kill embryos.
  • Disinfect the room where the incubator sits. Keep it clean, dry, and free of dust.
  • Quarantine hatched chicks for 2 weeks. Keep them in a separate brooder away from adult birds until you are sure they are healthy.

Value Addition & Packaging

Once you master hatching, you can increase your income by selling more than just chicks:

  • Day-old chicks: UGX 3,500-5,000 each (depending on breed and quality)
  • 1-month brooded chicks: UGX 8,000-10,000 each (saves the buyer the risky brooding stage)
  • Point-of-lay pullets: UGX 20,000-35,000 each (ready to start laying in 2-4 weeks)
  • Hatching eggs: UGX 500-1,000 each (sell to other farmers who want to hatch their own)
  • Incubator services: Charge other farmers UGX 200-300 per egg to hatch their eggs for them. This turns your incubator into a business.
💡 Packaging Tip: When selling chicks, use clean cardboard boxes with ventilation holes. Line the bottom with paper or wood shavings. Include a printed care sheet (feed type, vaccination schedule, temperature requirements). Professional packaging justifies premium prices and builds your reputation.
