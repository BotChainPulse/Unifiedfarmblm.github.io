5 Common Poultry Diseases in Uganda: Prevention & Treatment

Disease is the single biggest threat to poultry farmers in Uganda. In my first year at Unified Farm BLM, we lost 34 birds to Newcastle disease — a devastating blow that cost us UGX 1,020,000 in lost stock and medication. That experience taught me that prevention is always cheaper than treatment. This guide covers the five diseases every Ugandan poultry farmer must know, with symptoms, treatments, and prevention strategies that actually work.

Critical Rule: At the first sign of disease, isolate sick birds immediately. One infected chicken can wipe out your entire flock within 48 hours.

1. Newcastle Disease (NCD)

⚠️ The Deadliest Disease in Ugandan Poultry

Newcastle disease is a viral infection that spreads rapidly and kills within 48–72 hours. It is the number one cause of flock destruction in Uganda.

Symptoms to Watch For:
  • Sudden death with no warning signs
  • Difficulty breathing and gasping
  • Greenish diarrhea
  • Twisted necks (torticollis)
  • Drooping wings and paralysis
  • Swelling around the eyes and neck

Treatment

There is no cure for Newcastle disease. Infected birds must be culled immediately and buried deep (at least 2 meters) to prevent spreading. The entire coop must be disinfected with strong bleach solution or phenolic disinfectant.

Prevention

  • Vaccinate every bird: Use Lasota vaccine (eye drop) at day 1, day 14, and month 3. Repeat every 3–4 months.
  • Biosecurity: Restrict visitor access to your coop. Anyone entering must change shoes.
  • Quarantine new birds: Keep new chickens separate for 14 days before introducing them to your flock.
  • Cost: UGX 500 – 1,000 per bird for full vaccination schedule.

2. Gumboro (Infectious Bursal Disease)

⚠️ Targets Young Chicks Specifically

Gumboro attacks the immune system of chicks aged 3–6 weeks, making them vulnerable to every other disease.

Symptoms to Watch For:
  • Watery, whitish diarrhea
  • Depression and huddling together
  • Loss of appetite
  • Feather picking
  • Sudden death in apparently healthy chicks

Treatment

There is no specific treatment. Supportive care includes electrolytes in drinking water, vitamin supplements, and keeping chicks warm. Antibiotics may prevent secondary infections.

Prevention

  • Vaccinate at day 1: Use intermediate-plus Gumboro vaccine via eye drop or drinking water.
  • Boost at day 14–18: Second dose ensures full protection.
  • Strict sanitation: Gumboro virus survives in litter for months. Change bedding regularly.
  • Cost: UGX 800 – 1,500 per bird for complete protection.

3. Fowl Pox

⚠️ Slow but Damaging

Fowl pox is a viral disease spread by mosquitoes. It causes wart-like growths on the comb, wattles, and eyelids, reducing feed intake and growth.

Symptoms to Watch For:
  • Yellowish wart-like bumps on comb and wattles
  • Scabby lesions around the eyes and beak
  • Reduced feed consumption
  • Difficulty breathing (if wet form affects throat)
  • Drop in egg production

Treatment

Most birds recover naturally in 2–4 weeks. Apply iodine solution to lesions to prevent secondary infection. For severe cases, consult a veterinarian.

Prevention

  • Vaccinate at 4–8 weeks: Wing-web stab method.
  • Mosquito control: Eliminate standing water near the coop. Use mosquito netting on windows.
  • Isolate infected birds: Fowl pox spreads slowly, so early isolation prevents outbreaks.
  • Cost: UGX 600 – 1,200 per bird.

4. Coccidiosis

⚠️ The Silent Profit Killer

Coccidiosis is caused by protozoa in damp, dirty litter. It damages the intestines, causing poor growth and death in young birds.

Symptoms to Watch For:
  • Bloody diarrhea (classic sign)
  • Ruffled feathers and lethargy
  • Pale comb and wattles (anemia)
  • Weight loss despite eating
  • Huddling in corners

Treatment

Use coccidiostats in drinking water: Amprolium, Coxid, or Sulfa drugs. Treat for 5–7 days. Provide vitamin K to prevent bleeding.

Prevention

  • Dry litter: The single most effective prevention. Wet litter = coccidiosis.
  • Prophylactic medication: Add coccidiostat to starter feed for the first 8 weeks.
  • Elevated feeders and drinkers: Prevent contamination of feed and water with droppings.
  • Cost: UGX 300 – 800 per bird for prevention.

5. Fowl Typhoid

⚠️ Persistent and Hard to Eliminate

Fowl typhoid is a bacterial infection (Salmonella) that causes chronic illness and reduces productivity across the entire flock.

Symptoms to Watch For:
  • Yellow-green diarrhea
  • High fever and thirst
  • Purple discoloration of comb and wattles
  • Weakness and difficulty walking
  • Reduced egg production or poor hatchability

Treatment

Use broad-spectrum antibiotics: Enrofloxacin, Neocycin, or Tetracycline in drinking water for 5–7 days. Cull severely infected birds.

Prevention

  • Buy vaccinated stock: Only purchase chicks from reputable hatcheries with health certificates.
  • Rodent control: Rats and mice spread Salmonella. Set traps around the coop.
  • Clean water: Change drinking water daily. Never let birds drink from stagnant sources.
  • Cost: UGX 400 – 1,000 per bird.
