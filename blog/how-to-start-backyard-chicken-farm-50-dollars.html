The 90-Day Backyard Poultry Starter Plan (Uganda Budget Edition)

Most beginners do not fail at poultry because of disease or feed prices. They fail because they start without a calendar. They buy birds before building shelter, spend the first egg money instead of saving it, and quit around day 60 — one month before the farm starts paying back.

This plan fixes that. It is a 90-day calendar for starting a small backyard flock in Uganda with roughly UGX 190,000–230,000, broken into four phases. Follow the phases in order and by day 90 you will have eggs selling every week, clean records, and a clear decision about what to do next.

Phase 1 (Days 1–7): Prepare Before You Buy a Single Bird

Resist the urge to buy chickens on day one. One week of preparation prevents most beginner disasters.

  • Shelter: 4–6 hens need only about 1 × 2 metres of covered, dry space. Eucalyptus poles, offcut timber and papyrus or iron-sheet roofing are enough. What matters: a roof that does not leak, walls that block dogs and wild cats at night, and air movement so the space stays dry.
  • Water plan: decide where clean water comes from every single day. A 5-litre drinker (UGX 8,000–12,000) serves 5 hens.
  • Feed plan: know the price of layer mash and maize bran at your nearest supplier before you own birds. Feed is your biggest running cost — go in with real numbers.
  • Supplier shortlist: identify two or three sources of point-of-lay birds — established hatcheries like Ugachick or Biyinzika outlets, or a trusted farmer whose flock you can see. Ask about vaccination history before paying.

Phase 2 (Days 8–30): Settle the Flock

  • Buy 4–6 point-of-lay hens (UGX 25,000–35,000 each). For a first flock, hardy birds beat fancy birds: Kuroiler or improved local breeds forgive beginner mistakes that would kill a fragile exotic layer.
  • Expect a laying dip in week one. Moved birds are stressed birds. Production drops for a few days, then returns. Do not panic and do not start changing feed.
  • Fix the daily rhythm: feed and water first thing in the morning, egg collection mid-morning and late afternoon, secure the shelter at dusk. Fifteen minutes, same times, every day.
  • Start the record book on day 8, not day 30. One line a day: eggs collected, feed used, anything unusual. This notebook becomes your most valuable farm tool in phase 4.

Phase 3 (Days 31–60): Turn Eggs into Shillings

  • Sell deliberately. Tell neighbours, your boda rider, your church or mosque group, and one or two nearby shops. A tray sold to a regular weekly customer is worth more than a tray sold to a stranger at a higher price.
  • Separate farm money from house money. Use a mobile money line or a tin that is only for the farm. Egg money goes in; feed money comes out. What remains is the truth about whether the farm works.
  • The 70% rule: for these first two months, at least 70% of farm profit stays in the farm — for the next feed bag, then for the expansion you will choose in phase 4.

Phase 4 (Days 61–90): Read Your Numbers, Choose Your Next Move

By day 60 your record book can answer the only question that matters: is this working? Then you pick one of three paths:

PathChoose It IfNext Step
More layersEggs sell out every week and customers ask for moreAdd 5–10 point-of-lay hens; expand shelter first
Add a broiler batchYou want faster, larger cash injectionsPlan 25–50 broilers as a separate 7-week project with its own budget
Hatch your ownYou have a good rooster and patient temperamentStart with a broody hen before considering any incubator

If eggs are not selling, the answer is not more chickens — it is more customers. Spend phase 4 finding buyers before adding a single bird.

Where the money comes back: five settled hens give roughly 3–5 eggs a day. At UGX 400–500 per egg, that is UGX 36,000–75,000 per month — enough to cover ongoing feed from around month two, with the surplus building your expansion fund.

Time Your Start with Uganda's Seasons

When you start matters almost as much as how:

  • Best months to start: just after the main harvests (roughly July–August and December–January), when maize bran and feed ingredients are cheapest.
  • Rainy seasons: greens and insects are abundant — free feed. But double-check your roof and floor drainage before the rains, not during.
  • Dry seasons: water becomes the daily risk. Budget for it and never let drinkers run dry — even a few hours without water cuts egg production for days.
