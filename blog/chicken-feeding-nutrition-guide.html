Chicken Feeding & Nutrition: The Complete Guide for Ugandan Farmers

Feed is the single biggest expense in chicken farming — making up 70-80% of your total costs. But it's also the single biggest factor in how many eggs your hens produce, how fast your broilers grow, and whether your birds stay healthy. Get feeding right, and your farm profits. Get it wrong, and even the best chickens will fail.

Why Nutrition is Your #1 Priority

Many beginner farmers make the mistake of thinking "chickens eat anything." While chickens are opportunistic feeders, what they eat directly determines:

  • Egg production: A poorly fed hen lays 2-3 eggs per week. A well-fed hen lays 5-6.
  • Egg quality: Thin shells, pale yolks, and small eggs all come from poor nutrition.
  • Bird health: Malnourished birds have weak immune systems and get sick easily.
  • Growth rate: Broilers need precise nutrition to reach market weight in 6-7 weeks.
  • Lifespan: Properly fed layers can produce for 2-3 years. Poorly fed birds burn out in 12 months.
💡 The Golden Rule: You cannot save money by buying cheap feed. Cheap feed means low protein, poor minerals, and fillers. Your hens will lay fewer eggs, grow slower, and get sick more often. The "savings" on feed cost will be lost 10 times over in reduced production.

Feed by Life Stage: What, When, and How Much

Chickens need different nutrition at different ages. Feeding layer mash to day-old chicks is like feeding steak to a baby — it won't work and may cause harm.

Chicks (0-6 Weeks): Starter Feed

  • Protein requirement: 20-22%
  • Feed type: Starter mash or crumbles (crumbles are easier for small beaks)
  • Daily amount: Fill feeders and let them eat freely (ad libitum). Chicks need constant access.
  • Water: Critical. Use shallow chick drinkers to prevent drowning. Change daily.
  • Calcium: Low (about 1%). Too much calcium at this age damages kidneys.
⚠️ Never feed layer mash to chicks under 18 weeks. The high calcium content causes gout (kidney failure) and permanent organ damage. This is one of the most common beginner mistakes.

Growers (6-18 Weeks): Grower Feed

  • Protein requirement: 16-18%
  • Feed type: Grower mash or pellets
  • Daily amount: 80-100g per bird per day. Measure it — overfeeding causes obesity.
  • Purpose: Supports steady growth without pushing too fast. Overfeeding at this stage causes fat birds that lay poorly later.

Layers (18+ Weeks): Layer Mash

  • Protein requirement: 16-18%
  • Calcium requirement: 3.5-4% (this is critical for strong eggshells)
  • Feed type: Layer mash or pellets (mash is cheaper, pellets reduce waste)
  • Daily amount: 110-120g per bird per day. Adjust for weather — birds eat less in extreme heat.
  • Supplements: Offer crushed oyster shells or eggshells in a separate container for extra calcium.

Broilers (Meat Chickens): Broiler Feed

  • Protein requirement: 20-22% for maximum growth
  • Feed type: Broiler starter (week 1-3), broiler finisher (week 4-6)
  • Daily amount: Ad libitum (unlimited access). Broilers are bred to eat constantly.
  • Timeline: Market weight (2-2.5 kg) reached at 6-7 weeks with proper feeding.
📊 Quick Reference Table

Chicks (0-6 weeks): 20-22% protein, 1% calcium, free feeding
Growers (6-18 weeks): 16-18% protein, 1% calcium, 80-100g/day
Layers (18+ weeks): 16-18% protein, 3.5-4% calcium, 110-120g/day
Broilers (0-7 weeks): 20-22% protein, 1% calcium, free feeding

Types of Feed Available in Uganda

Not all feed is equal. Here's what you'll find in Ugandan agro-input shops and what to look for:

Commercial Mash (Recommended for Beginners)

  • Brands: Unga Feeds, Bunyoro Feeds, Kapeeka Agro, local millers
  • Form: Loose powder (mash) or compressed pellets
  • Pros: Complete nutrition, consistent quality, convenient
  • Cons: More expensive than home mixing, some brands use low-quality ingredients
  • Price: UGX 1,800-2,500 per kg for layer mash (2026 prices)

Home-Mixed Rations (For Experienced Farmers)

  • Ingredients: Maize bran, silver fish (mukene), soybeans, premix vitamins, limestone, bone meal
  • Pros: 20-30% cheaper than commercial feed if you have ingredient sources
  • Cons: Requires precise formulation. Get the protein ratio wrong and your birds suffer. Not recommended for beginners.
⚠️ Don't mix your own feed until you understand the math. You need to calculate protein content, amino acids, calcium, phosphorus, and vitamins. A slight mistake in formulation can cause deficiency diseases or death. Use commercial feed for your first 6-12 months, then consider mixing once you have experience.

Kitchen Scraps: What You Can and Cannot Feed

Kitchen scraps can supplement commercial feed and reduce costs by 10-20%. But some common foods are toxic to chickens.

✅ Safe Scraps (In Moderation)

  • Vegetables: Cabbage, spinach, sukuma wiki, carrot tops, pumpkin, cucumber peels, sweet potato leaves
  • Grains: Rice, leftover ugali, pasta, bread (small amounts), cooked beans
  • Fruits: Banana peels, watermelon rinds, mango peels (not too much — high sugar)
  • Protein: Leftover fish, meat scraps, termites, grasshoppers (excellent protein!)
  • Dairy: Small amounts of yogurt or sour milk (good for gut bacteria)

❌ NEVER Feed These

  • Avocado: All parts (fruit, skin, pit) are toxic. Can cause heart failure and death within hours.
  • Chocolate / Caffeine: Contains theobromine, toxic to birds.
  • Onions / Garlic (in large amounts): Small amounts of garlic in water are fine. Large amounts of onions destroy red blood cells.
  • Raw Beans: Contains phytohaemagglutinin — toxic unless thoroughly cooked.
  • Salty / Processed Foods: Chips, crisps, heavily salted leftovers — causes salt poisoning and kidney damage.
  • Moldy Food: Mold produces aflatoxins that kill birds. Never feed anything you wouldn't eat yourself.
  • Citrus Peels: Can cause digestive issues in large quantities.
🍽️ The 10% Rule: Kitchen scraps should make up no more than 10% of your chickens' total diet. Commercial feed provides the complete nutrition they need. Scraps are a supplement, not a replacement. If scraps exceed 10%, your hens will produce fewer eggs and weaker shells.

Feed Storage Rules: Don't Let Good Feed Go Bad

Bad storage ruins expensive feed. Moldy feed is not just wasted money — it can kill your entire flock. Follow these rules:

  • Dry: Store in a waterproof container or room. Humidity causes mold in 48 hours during rainy season.
  • Sealed: Use airtight containers or heavy-duty bags. Oxygen degrades vitamins and fats.
  • Elevated: Keep bags off the floor on pallets or wood. Floor moisture rises into feed.
  • Cool: Store in a shaded, ventilated area. Heat destroys vitamins and causes rancidity.
  • First in, first out: Use older feed before opening new bags. Mark purchase dates with a marker.
  • Check before feeding: If feed smells musty, has visible mold, or clumps together, throw it away. Never risk it.
⚠️ Moldy Feed = Dead Birds. Aflatoxin poisoning from moldy feed causes liver failure, reduces egg production, and kills slowly. If you see blue, green, or white mold on feed, bury it. The loss of one bag of feed is nothing compared to losing your flock.
